---
title: "Mission"
---

The **Tester Mission** is a short, clear instruction describing what you want the AI tester to achieve. It's one of the most important field in the builder - it defines the goal of the session and keeps every tester's feedback focused and actionable.

The mission is always required, and the more specific and granular you make it, the more targeted the results will be toward the flow you plan to analyze.

## Write it like a single concrete task

Good missions describe one goal with a clear end state:

> _"Complete the registration flow using a social login."_

> _"Upgrade your plan from free to premium using a Mastercard credit card."_

> _"Find and purchase two tickets for tonight's 9:00 PM showing."_

## Tips for effective missions

<Tip>
  **Lead with the goal, not the steps.** Write "Find a plan that fits your budget and sign up" rather than "Click Pricing, select Pro, then fill in your details." Step-by-step instructions train testers to follow directions instead of revealing where your design actually fails.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Don’t tell testers how to give feedback.** You don’t need to ask testers to “think out loud,” “share first impressions,” or explain what they like or find confusing. Uxia testers already provide thoughts, reactions, and feedback throughout every session.

  Keep the mission focused on the task.

  Instead of:

  “Take a quick look at the page, share your first impression, then purchase a Macbook and tell us what you are thinking.”

  Write:

  “Explore the laptops catalogue and purchase a Macbook.”
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Avoid leading language.** Don't name UI elements ("click the blue button"), imply a correct path ("find the easy way to…"), or signal what testers should feel ("notice how simple it is…"). Neutral wording is everything.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **One mission, one primary goal.** If you need to test several flows, run several tests. Compound missions produce muddled results.
</Tip>

<Note>
  Each field has a **Review** helper on the top right of the field. This button can help you get more ideas on how to frame your mission.
</Note>

## Example

Suppose your business objective is:

> **"_Understand whether customers can successfully purchase a new mobile phone plan._"**

It may be tempting to test the entire journey with one mission:

<Warning>
  **Too Broad**

  "_Find a mobile plan that fits your needs, create an account, purchase the plan, and contact support if you have any questions._"
</Warning>

Although this sounds like a realistic customer journey, it combines several different tasks into a single mission:

- Finding the right plan
- Creating an account
- Completing the purchase
- Asking for help or contacting support

If testers struggle, it becomes much harder to understand which part of the experience caused the friction.

Instead, split the objective into separate tests and missions:

**Mission 1 (Test 1) - Find a suitable plan**

<Check>
  "_Find a mobile plan that includes around 10 GB of monthly data and add it to your cart._"
</Check>

**Mission 2 (Test 2) - Create an account**

<Check>
  "_Create a new account to become a customer._"
</Check>

**Mission 3 (Test 3) - Purchase the plan**

<Check>
  "_Purchase the mobile plan you selected and add an additional product to your order._"
</Check>

**Mission 4 (Test 4) - Get help**

<Check>
  _"Find how to contact customer support to ask a question about your subscription."_
</Check>

Each mission measures one clear user goal. This makes the results easier to interpret and helps you identify exactly where users encounter friction.

<Tip>
  You can duplicate the tests and change only the Mission to help you set them up faster.
</Tip>
