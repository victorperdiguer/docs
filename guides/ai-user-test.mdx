Testing your prototype before writing a single line of production code is one of the highest-leverage moves in product design. When you catch confusion points, navigation dead-ends, and trust gaps at the prototype stage, you fix them in minutes — not after a sprint's worth of engineering work. Uxia's AI synthetic testers navigate your designs, think aloud as they go, and surface the issues your team is too close to see.

## Supported Formats

<Info>
  Uxia supports prototypes built in **Figma**, **Lovable**, **Base44**, **Cursor**, **Stitch**, and **Claude**. If your tool publishes a shareable URL, paste it directly into the test setup. For static screens or image exports, upload them as files during setup.
</Info>

You can test:

- **Figma prototypes** — paste your shareable Figma prototype link directly
- **Figma exports** — upload individual screen images or PDF exports
- **Other tool prototypes** — any publicly accessible prototype URL from the tools listed above
- **Static design screens** — JPG, PNG, or PDF uploads of design mockups

## Step-by-Step Guide

<Steps>
  <Step title="Create a new AI User Test">
    From your Uxia dashboard, click **New Test** and select **AI User Test**. Give your test a name that reflects the design version or hypothesis you're testing — this makes it easy to compare runs later.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Upload your prototype or paste the Figma link">
    In the test setup, paste your Figma prototype URL or upload your static design files. If your prototype requires a starting screen, make sure the link points directly to that frame. Uxia's testers will begin navigation from wherever the link or first image lands.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Define your audience">
    Describe who you want to test with. Set demographics (age range, location, occupation), tech literacy level, and any persona characteristics that match your real target users. The closer your synthetic audience matches your actual users, the more actionable your results will be.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Write the mission and scenario">
    Tell testers what they should try to accomplish — not how to do it. A mission gives context and a goal; the tester figures out the path. This is where the most useful think-aloud data comes from. See the tips below for writing effective missions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Select your testers">
    Review the available synthetic tester profiles. Each profile includes demographic details, behavioral tendencies, and tech experience. Pick 5–10 testers whose profiles match the range of users you want to represent. A wider spread surfaces more diverse issues.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Launch the test">
    Click **Launch**. Uxia's AI testers begin their sessions immediately. Most tests complete end-to-end in under 20 minutes.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Read transcripts and insights report">
    When the test finishes, open the **Insights Report** for a synthesized summary of findings, UX benchmark scores (SUS, SUPR-Q, and the Uxia Optimization Score), and prioritized issues. Dive into individual **think-aloud transcripts** to see exactly what each tester said and did at every step.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Tips for Writing Effective Missions

<Tip>
  **Lead with the goal, not the steps.** Write "Find a plan that fits your budget and sign up" rather than "Click the Pricing tab, then select the Pro plan, then fill in your details." Step-by-step instructions train testers to follow instructions instead of revealing where your design fails.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Use realistic scenarios.** Give testers a believable context: "You're looking for a project management tool for a team of five. You've landed on this product's homepage." Grounding the task in a real situation produces more authentic reactions and better think-aloud data.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Avoid leading language.** Don't mention UI elements by name ("click the blue button"), imply a correct path ("find the easy way to…"), or signal what you expect them to feel ("notice how simple it is to…"). Neutral wording is everything.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **One mission, one primary goal.** If you need to test multiple flows, run multiple tests or use multiple tasks within a single test. Compound missions produce muddled results.
</Tip>

## What to Look for in Results

When you review transcripts and the insights report, focus on these signal types:

| Signal | What It Looks Like |
| --- | --- |
| **Confusion points** | Testers pause, re-read labels, or express uncertainty about where to go next |
| **Drop-off moments** | Testers give up, declare they would leave, or restart from the top |
| **Unexpected paths** | Testers navigate to screens in an order you didn't anticipate |
| **Trust issues** | Testers question credibility, data safety, or pricing transparency |
| **Positive reinforcement** | Testers move quickly and confidently through a flow — note what worked |

Pay special attention to moments where multiple testers hit the same wall. A single tester's confusion could be a profile quirk; three testers failing the same step is a design problem.
