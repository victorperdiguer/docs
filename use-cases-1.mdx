---
title: "Use Cases"
---

An Accessibility Test earns its place whenever you want to catch accessibility issues while they're still cheap to fix — at the design stage, before implementation locks them in. A few situations where it's particularly useful:

**Pre-development design review.** Audit product screens against WCAG 2.2 before handoff, so accessibility problems surface in design rather than late in QA or after code ships.

**Splitting work between designers and engineers.** Every issue is tagged as a likely [design or code fix](/criteria-evaluation), giving both teams a shared checklist and a clean way to divide the work.

**Compliance-driven reviews.** When you need to show you've checked against a recognized standard — AA as the practical, legally-aligned baseline, or AAA when you're aiming for the highest coverage.

**Tracking progress over time.** Re-run the test after fixes to confirm issues are resolved and watch the pass, fail, and review counts move in the right direction.

<Note>
  This isn't a substitute for a formal legal audit, specialist review, or certification — it's an early-and-often check that keeps accessibility debt from piling up.
</Note>
