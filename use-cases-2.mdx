title: "Use Cases"
---

AI User Research is for the moments when you need to understand what people think — before you build, between iterations, or when behavioral data alone doesn't explain what's going on. A few situations where it earns its place:

**Concept and idea validation.** Pressure-test product directions, feature ideas, and value propositions before you commit engineering time — the most expensive mistake in product development is building the wrong thing with confidence. Describe an idea or share a rough concept in plain language, ask a matched synthetic audience how they'd react, and get directional signal before you invest in design or development.

**Messaging and copy.** Find the framing that lands: value propositions, taglines, landing-page headlines, feature names and labels, and pricing framing. Put several options head-to-head to see which resonates — and whether your terminology means to users what you think it means.

**Understanding your audience.** Compare how different segments respond to the same prompt, uncover needs your current product isn't addressing, and surface the language and mental models your real users bring — the raw material for sharper positioning and copy.

**Explaining the _why_ behind your data.** When analytics or an [AI User Test](/about) show you _what_ is happening but not _why_, research fills the gap by asking testers directly about the attitudes and motivations behind the behavior.

<Note>
  **Where it's not the right fit:** AI User Research is attitudinal — it captures what testers think and prefer, not how they act in a live interface. If you need to see where users get stuck, misclick, or drop off as they navigate, reach for an [AI User Test](/about) instead. The two pair well: research to understand opinions, testing to see how they play out in practice.
</Note>
