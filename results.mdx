---
title: "Results"
---

The **Results** tab is the main summary screen for an AI User Test. It moves from a high-level synthesis down to the evidence: an executive summary, the ranked insights and recommendations, the structured issue report, and the satisfaction scores.

## Executive Summary

The tab opens with an **Executive Summary** card - a short narrative covering the whole test: what testers were trying to do, what worked well, the main problems found, and the recommended next steps. In a ticket-purchase test, for example, it might call out easy ticket discovery and clear pricing as strengths, then flag long onboarding, confusing receipt wording, and an external payment handoff as the bigger issues.

## Top Insights & Next Steps

Below the summary, the findings split into two short ranked lists:

- **Top Insights** - the most important problems found, e.g. a long onboarding flow, unclear 1-hour validity rules, or a payment redirect that feels unsafe.
- **Next Steps** - those findings turned into direct recommendations, e.g. add a prominent fast path to buying a ticket, clarify the validity rules, surface payment methods earlier.

Keeping them side by side separates diagnosis from action.

## Insights Report

The **Insights Report** is the main structured issue list. It opens with a few headline metrics - total insights, total categories, and the **Usability Optimization Score™ (UOS)** - then a table of every finding.

The UOS is a single summary score based on the test results, combining identified insights, qualitative feedback, and user satisfaction across the same four UX pillars used in [User Satisfaction](#user-satisfaction) - Usability, Trust, Appearance, and Loyalty. It's shown with a rating and a value, for example **Good - 72%**.

Each finding is organized by Insight, Relevance, Frequency, and Category:

| Insight | Relevance | Frequency | Category |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Onboarding flow is too long before the first ticket | High | Occasional | Usability |
| 1-hour ticket validity rules are unclear | High | Occasional | Copy |
| Payment redirect appears in another language and feels unsafe | Medium | Rare | Trust |

<Note>
  The rows above are an example. Your own report is generated from your test's findings.
</Note>

### Relevance

Relevance indicates the impact level of an issue:

| Label | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **High / Medium / Low** | The impact level of the issue on the experience |
| **Prototype** | The issue may come from the prototype itself and may not reflect a final shipped product |

### Frequency

Frequency groups issues by how often testers mentioned them, so you can tell an isolated quirk from a broadly repeated problem:

| Band | Share of testers |
| --- | --- |
| **Rare** | 0–20% |
| **Occasional** | 21–50% |

### Category

Each issue is also tagged by the kind of UX problem it represents - for example **Navigation**, **Copy**, **Trust**, **Usability**, or **CTAs**.

## Insight detail drawer

Every row has a **Show Details** action that opens a right-side drawer for that single issue. The drawer carries the insight title, its relevance badge, category tags, and frequency marker, plus:

- **Overview**, **Testers Evidence**, and **Fix Suggestion** tabs
- **Affected Frames** - the exact screens where the issue showed up
- A **Mark as prototype** control

The **Overview** tab holds a longer explanation and an **Impact Assessment** split into **User Impact** and **Business Impact**. Because each finding ties back to the affected frames, the report stays visual - you see where a problem happened, not just a description of it.

## User Satisfaction

Further down, the **User Satisfaction** area pairs standard UX scoring with a short AI-written interpretation. It surfaces the **UXIA-Q Score**, the **System Usability Scale (SUS)**, and pillar tabs for Usability, Trust, Appearance, and Loyalty.

SUS is reported against the common benchmark of 68:

| Score | Value | Benchmark |
| --- | --- | --- |
| SUS | 74.5 | 68 |

Each pillar tab shows a benchmark delta, an average score out of 5, question-level results, and a plain-language summary - for example, a Usability view reading "Quite easy to use" alongside an AI paragraph explaining the reasons behind the score. It's a useful spot for teams that want both a numeric signal and a readable explanation.
