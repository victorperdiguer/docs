Questions are the core of an AI User Research study. Instead of navigating an interface, each [synthetic tester](/audiences/overview) answers the questions you write, so the way you phrase and structure them shapes everything you get back. The builder lets you mix formats in a single run — closed questions for quantifiable signal, open ones for depth.

## Building your questions

Add up to **10 questions** per study. Every question, regardless of type, shares the same building blocks:

- **Question text** — the prompt the tester answers.
- **Additional context** _(optional)_ — extra framing or instructions that guide how testers should interpret the question, without cluttering the prompt itself.
- **Images** _(optional)_ — attach one or more visuals when you want testers to react to a concept, screen, or design rather than text alone.

Each question card also has **reorder**, **duplicate**, and **delete** controls, so you can restructure the flow, reuse a well-worded question as a starting point, or trim anything that isn't earning its place.

<Tip>
  Lead with broad, open questions and narrow toward specifics later. Asking for a first impression before you reveal defined options keeps early answers from being anchored by the choices you show.
</Tip>

## The six question types

Pick a type per question based on the kind of signal you're after.

### Yes / No

A fixed binary response — the tester answers yes or no, with no extra setup beyond the question itself. Best for a clean, unambiguous read on a single claim, feature, or value proposition.

### Rating Scale

Quantify sentiment or satisfaction on a numeric scale. Choose one of three preset ranges — **1–5**, **1–7**, or **1–10** — and provide required labels for the low and high ends (for example, "Very difficult" → "Very easy") so testers and readers share the same anchor points.

### Multiple Choice

Present a defined set of options and see which resonate. Choose between:

- **Single-select** — the tester picks exactly one option.
- **Multi-select** — the tester can pick several.

Define between **2 and 10 options** per question.

### Open-ended

A free-form text response with no structure beyond the prompt. This is where you uncover motivations, unexpected reactions, and the _why_ behind behavior — testers describe experiences and explain their reasoning in their own words.

### Ranking

Ask testers to order a set of items by preference or priority. Provide labels for the **top** and **bottom** of the ranking, then define **2 to 8 items** to be ranked. Useful for forcing trade-offs between options that would each score well on their own.

### Budget Allocation

Give testers **100 points** to distribute across **2 to 8 items** by importance. Because the points are finite, this surfaces relative priority more honestly than rating each item in isolation — testers have to decide what genuinely matters most.

<Note>
  Ranking and Budget Allocation both measure relative preference, but they answer different questions. Ranking gives you a strict order; Budget Allocation shows you _how much_ more one item matters than another.
</Note>

## Choosing the right mix

| You want to know… | Reach for |
| --- | --- |
| A clear signal on one claim | Yes / No |
| How strongly testers feel | Rating Scale |
| Which option wins | Multiple Choice |
| The reasoning behind a response | Open-ended |
| The order of preference | Ranking |
| Where priorities really sit | Budget Allocation |
