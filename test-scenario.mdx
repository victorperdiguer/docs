The **Test Scenario** is a backstory that gives your mission context. Where the [mission](/mission) says _what_ the tester should do, the scenario says _what's happening in their life_ that leads them to do it. It helps testers imagine a realistic situation, so their behavior lands closer to a real user's than a task-follower's.

## What a good scenario does

A scenario sets the stage without prescribing actions. For example:

> "You've just moved to another country and want to open a bank account to receive your salary."
>
> "You're evaluating project management tools for a team of five and have just landed on this product's homepage."

Neither one tells the tester what to click - they establish motivation and mindset. That framing produces more authentic reactions and richer think-aloud data and more realistic tester concerns.

<Tip>
  Keep the scenario short and grounded in a believable situation. One or two sentences of context is enough - you don't need a full persona here, since that's what your [audience](/concepts/ai-participants) is for.
</Tip>

<Warning>
  **Differentiate between Mission, Scenario, Stop Condition and Audience**

  While the Mission represents the task (the "What"), the Audience represents who is performing that task (the "Who"), the Stop Condition is when to stop the test (the "Until when"), and the Scenario represents the situational context (the "Why" or the circumstances in which the task is performed).

  For example:

  **Mission**: Purchase a 1-hour travel ticket in this subway app.

  **Scenario**: You have just landed in New York for the first time for a 10-day summer holiday, and you need a way to get around the city using the subway.

  **Stop Condition**: Stop when you reach the credit card information screen.

  **Audience**: Male or female, 25-40 years old, born in the UK, full-time employed, etc.
</Warning>
